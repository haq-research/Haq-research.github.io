Enhancing Decision-Making in LLM-as-a-Judge Systems

We’ve seen growing adoption of LLMs-as-a-Judge across domains like summarization, question answering, and medical annotation — often paired with human annotators in a Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) workflow. However, this hybrid approach brings its own set of challenges, especially in high-stakes, domain-specific settings like healthcare. Recently, I came across a fantastic paper from University of Cambridge and Apple: “Can External Validation Tools Improve Annotation Quality for LLM-as-a-Judge?”

The paper outlines the limitations faced by human annotators, even when assisted by LLMs: – Overweighting writing quality over factual accuracy – Limited domain expertise, especially in technical subjects – Difficulty evaluating long-form factual answers – Challenges in contentious or subjective topics

In our current project in the medical domain, we’re exploring an enhanced approach: Agentic Tools + LLM-as-a-Judge + HITL

Inspired by the paper’s findings, we’ve incorporated external agents into the evaluation loop:

  1. Initial Domain Assistant — Offers structured summaries, guidelines, or definitions
  2. Validation Tools — Fact-checkers, retrieval agents, or confidence scorers
  3. Final Decision Agent — Consolidates inputs before making a final ruling

This layered decision system improves annotation precision, factual consistency, and evaluator confidence.

That said, agentic approaches come with their own limitations: Performance may regress in out-of-domain tasks – Ambiguity in domains where expert consensus is weak – Prompting strategies and pairing methods can significantly skew outcomes

Still, the promise is clear: Multi-agent, tool-augmented LLMs can raise the bar in judgment quality — especially in mission-critical domains.

Curious to hear how others are integrating agents, evaluators, and domain tools into their LLM workflows!
